Cellphone use causes very few of Chicago's traffic crashes.

But crashes caused by cellphone use are almost twice as dangerous as the average crash.

By Arthi Venkatesh
Last updated March 2025

In Chicago, there were more than 60,000 traffic crashes in the last six months alone. Traffic crashes are a leading cause of death in the United States — they kill over 120 people every day, according to the CDC.

Why Do People Crash?

Understanding the reasons why people get into traffic accidents is the first step to lowering crash rates, making our roads more safe.

The city of Chicago provides data on citywide traffic crashes, with information from the Chicago Police Department on conditions surrounding the crash, location, cause and more. After analyzing that data, we found that the most common primary contributory cause to traffic crashes over the last six months was failure to yield right of way.

According to the Illinois General Assembly, the police considers a crash to have been caused by failure to yield right of way if a driver is "involved in a collision at an intersection or interferes with the movement of other vehicles after driving past a yield right-of-way sign," like a stop sign or yield sign.

Crashes Caused by Cellphone Use

Very few crashes over the last six months were primarily attributed to cellphone use — only about 0.14% of traffic crashes in the last six months were directly attributed to cellphone use (i.e. texting or other cellphone use). However, it is important to note that causes of crashes were only known in about 60% of cases. This means that it is possible that a greater proportion of crashes were actually caused by cellphone use.

Nevertheless, we can see this trend carry through across years — the proportion of traffic crashes that were directly attributed to cellphone use tops out at about 0.2% in 2019 and has generally been decreasing in the years since.

Despite this trend, campaigns to reduce texting and driving are commonplace in Chicago and across the nation. For example, there has been a concerted effort on the part of the Illinois Secretary of State’s office to raise awareness about distracted driving.

Illinois Secretary of State Alexi Giannoulias’s office launched a campaign last October called “One Road. One Focus” that includes a new requirement for teens with a learner's permit to watch a video on the consequences of distracted driving before they receive their driver’s license.

Concerns around cellphone use while driving may stem not from the number of accidents cellphone use causes, but from the severity of accidents that were a result of cellphone use.

We found that crashes that were caused by cellphone use tended to result in significantly higher rates of injury. About 30% of those in cellphone-related crashes were injured, vs. about 16% across all types of crashes.

Ultimately, cellphone-related crashes are avoidable crashes. We saw that cellphone-related crash rates have been decreasing since 2019, possibly pointing to the efffectiveness of Chicago's efforts to curb distracted driving. Even at its lowest, though, there were still more than 150 cellphone-related crashes in Chicago.

State legislators have been active in introducing new policy surrounding distracted driving. Just last year, a bill went into affect that makes it illegal for people to participate in a video conference or access social media while driving.
